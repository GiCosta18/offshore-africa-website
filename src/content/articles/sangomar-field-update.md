When Sangomar delivered first oil, it marked a genuine inflection point: Senegal joined the ranks of African crude producers, and an offshore project years in the making finally began returning value. A year on, the more important story is consistency. The field has settled into stable output, and the production data is giving the operator and the state the confidence to plan the next phases.

Sangomar's subsea architecture was designed for a multi-phase campaign, with development wells tied back to a floating production, storage and offloading vessel. That design choice is now paying off. Infill drilling and water injection can sustain plateau production and extend the field's economic life without the cost of an entirely new surface facility.

For Petrosen, the national oil company, the past year has been an education in operatorship as much as ownership. Building technical and commercial capacity around a producing asset is the foundation for Senegal's ambition to take a more active role in future developments rather than remaining a passive partner.

The wider significance is fiscal. Sangomar revenues, alongside the GTA gas project, give Dakar a window to invest in power infrastructure, reduce reliance on imported fuels, and fund the social spending that justifies resource development in the eyes of citizens. Managing that revenue with transparency and discipline is now the central challenge — and the one by which the Sangomar era will ultimately be judged.
