The Grand Tortue Ahmeyim (GTA) project straddles the maritime border between Senegal and Mauritania, and it has become a rare example of two nations turning a shared resource into a shared opportunity rather than a dispute. With Phase 1 LNG cargoes now reaching international markets, both governments are weighing a Phase 2 expansion that would lift production volumes and extend the project's economic life well into the next decade.

Phase 2 is more than an incremental uplift. Engineering studies under review propose additional subsea wells, expanded processing capacity, and a debottlenecking of the floating LNG infrastructure already in place. The intercreditor and unitisation arrangements established for Phase 1 give the partners a tested framework, which should compress the timeline between sanction and start-up compared with a greenfield development.

The strategic logic is straightforward. Global LNG demand remains structurally firm, and buyers are actively seeking supply diversity outside traditional basins. A reliable, competitively priced West African source is attractive, and the GTA partners are positioning the field as a long-term anchor rather than a one-off project.

Domestically, both Dakar and Nouakchott are pressing for a larger share of gas to be allocated to power generation and industrial use. Gas-to-power schemes could displace expensive liquid fuels, stabilise electricity tariffs, and support the kind of value-added industry — fertiliser, processing, manufacturing — that converts a resource windfall into durable development. How the partners balance export economics against domestic priorities will be one of the defining negotiations of GTA Phase 2.
