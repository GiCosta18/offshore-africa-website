For decades, Nigeria's gas was an afterthought to its oil — much of it flared, stranded, or simply left in the ground. The country's current strategy reverses that hierarchy. Gas is now positioned as the centrepiece of an industrial and power agenda designed to outlast the volatility of the crude market.

The infrastructure programme is ambitious. Expanded trunkline capacity is intended to move gas from the producing south to demand centres further north, while new processing plants target the persistent problem of associated gas that has historically been flared. Each of these projects is as much about reliability as it is about volume: industrial investors need confidence that supply will not be interrupted.

> Africa's largest economy cannot industrialise on diesel generators. Gas is the bridge, and Nigeria is finally building it.

Domestic priorities sit at the heart of the plan. Gas-to-power projects, gas-based fertiliser production, and autogas programmes for transport all aim to keep more molecules at home rather than exporting raw value. The logic is to build a domestic market deep enough to justify continued upstream investment even when export prices soften.

Execution remains the open question. Funding gaps, security challenges along pipeline corridors, and the need for cost-reflective pricing have slowed previous gas initiatives. Whether this decade's expansion succeeds will depend less on the resource — which is abundant — and more on the institutional discipline to see long-dated infrastructure through to completion.
